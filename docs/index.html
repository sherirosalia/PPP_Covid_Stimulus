Unprecedented access to small business loan data

It seems that no section of the country was untouched by enterpreneurs seeking relief from the effects of the government shutdown in reaction to the Coronavirus. The release of lending records from the US Treasury in conjunction with the Small Business Association, albeit under unfortunate circumstances, allows an unusual glimpse into small businesses affected by the crisis.

Initial analysis

The purpose of the loans was to help businesses save jobs. The potential expenditure per capita of funds for the larger loan amounts came in at $2,000 and the under $150k disbursements at $8,000 per job saved. This assessment is based on calculations infered from statements Steve Mnuchin made, namely that the sum of the loans over $150k account for 75% of the total lent and was borrowed by only 13% of enterprises.

Machine learning also suggested a dependency between number of jobs "saved" and loan amounts as it gave this feature the most weight.

Even though the largest of loans went to seemingly the largest employers, the question arises as to how effective $2000 per job would be in helping employees in comparison to the smaller business loans which had a higher amount of money per person ratio.

Business loans and the jobs saved.

The pie charts below reflect the loan volume in each general job category along with the jobs saved. This helps to evaluate where the money went and which industries supported retention of the greatest number of jobs. For example, the visualizations suggest that the "Acommodation and Food Services" category saved the greatest percentage of employees in proportion to how much was borrowed and "Construction" looks to be the reverse.

The Banks

Bank of America and JP Morgan Chase were at the top of the list of lenders by count of loans, but financial tech organizations such as Kabbage and Celtic Bank made a strong showing.

Potential and limitations

The combination of geographic, demographic and loan information provides seemingly endless opportunities for deeper analysis. For example rural vs. urban job saved as a percentage of the local population. Regional differences in loan amount, bank activity and business types could project potential economic wastelands or growth spots. Additionally, how ethnic groups are integrating into the fabric of our economy could be gleaned from the data. For example, Asians borrowed almost twice as much as Hispanics while being one third the size of the Hispanic population. This may provide insight into cultural attitudes towards self employment, borrowing and proclivities towards certian industries. The fact that the NAICS codes were provided for almost all of the 5 million loans might be helpful to identify our most vulnerable industries and this could help to identify where to put efforts for retraining as automation progressses. It also seems like the data could also be helpful to identify industries and businesseses participating in money laundering.

Limitations of the data

We do not know the motivations of many of the businesses or how the money was spent. It was supposed to save jobs, but at a 1% interest rate and the prospect of forgiveness dangled as a part of the program, it is possible that a lot of this wound up in savings accounts or the stock market. In terms of whether or not the data acurately reflects the larger environment, we do not know who was unable to get the loans and who returned it. Some of the categories are to general to be helpful. For example in "Business Type", "Sole Proprietorship", "Self Employed" and "Limited Liability Corporation" could be a gig worker or an employer. As for the racial demographic information, only about 300k responded, and it is hard to say if this is a consistent sampling or not.

The aforementioned are just a few of the limitations and possibilities that this dataset holds and additionally, since the Paycheck Protection Program is still evolving there may be opportunity to add to the datasets.

A personal note

Small business is often touted as the "back bone" of America, and yet it seems that a mandate in reaction to a virus cut business owners "off at the knees". Even though this was mostly a pipeline project for me, compassion for the extreme nature of what small business people are experiencing led me to look at not only the general trends in an attempt to understand the effects that Covid is having on our economy but I also found myself being pulled into researching individual establishments of various categories and outliers. Some of my favorite restaurants, professional organizations, colleagues and friends appear in this database, and I am grateful for the support that this program afforded them and I hope it helps independent operators survive and thrive through the challenges from the pandemic.
