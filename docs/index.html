Cash for covid

Where did the Paycheck Protection Program money go?

Unprecedented access to small business loan data

It seems that no section of the country was untouched by enterpreneurs seeking relief from the effects of the government shutdown in reaction to the Coronavirus. The release of lending records from the US Treasury in conjunction with the Small Business Association, albeit under unfortunate circumstances, allows an unusual glimpse into small businesses affected by the crisis.

Wrangling the data

Over 50 CSV files were merged using a Python programming script to automate the process. Pyspark leveraged Hadoop to speed up processing 5 million loan records and application of random forest machine learning algorithm and visualizations were produced with Python Pandas. The full code and explanation of how to access the data records is in the repository linked below this paragraph.

GitHub repository: Cash for Covid, Clean Machine.

Data Source: Small Business Association.

The data and approach

The information came from the SBA in two formats: The largest dataset numbering 4.3 million rows provided loan amount information but the borrower names were withheld. Enterprise names were revealed for lending above $150k and the debt was put into categorical range such as $3 to $5 million instead of a specific tally.

Lending institution, business type and geographic location were consistent throughout both datasets. Approximately 5% of the borrowers supplied gender, racial identity and military status. Even though that is a small percentage relative to the rest of the information, it still provides almost a half of a million responses and the hope is that it represents general trends so it is preserved as an additional dataset.

Business type information came in the form of numerical NAICS codes which provided no context, fortunately information from US Census allowed the convert the keys into industry names and subcategories.

Initial analysis

The purpose of the loans was to help businesses save jobs. The potential expenditure per capita of funds for the larger loan amounts came in at $2,000 and the under $150k disbursements at $8,000 per job saved. This assessment is based on calculations infered from statements Steve Mnuchin made, namely that the sum of the loans over $150k account for 75% of the total lent and was borrowed by only 13% of enterprises.

Machine learning also suggested a dependency between number of jobs "saved" and loan ammounts as it gave this feature the most weight.

Even though the largest of loans went to seemingly the largest employers, the question arises as to how effective $2000 per job would be in helping employees in comparison to the smaller business loans which had a higher amount of money per person ratio.

Businesses loans and the jobs saved.

The pie charts below reflect the loan volume in each general job category along with the jobs saved. This helps to evaluate where the money went and which industries supported retention of the greatest number of jobs. For example, the visualizations suggest that the "Acommodation and Food Services" category saved the greatest percentage of employees in proportion to how much was borrowed and "Construction" looks to be the reverse.

The Banks

Bank of America and JP Morgan Chase were at the top of the list of lenders by count of loans, but financial tech organizations such as Kabbage and Celtic Bank made a strong showing.

The next step

There is a lot here, and the number of lines of inquiry seem potentially endless. A few ideas are to obtain granularity on loan dispersement per job saved in various categories, regional lending differences such as rural vs urban, differences between banks in various states.

One of many candidates for deeper analysis is a racially based observation for loans under $150k is that Asians borrowed almost twice as much as Hispanics did while only being one third the size of the Hispanic population. There could be any number of reasons for this but examining rates of self employment, business types and/or cultural attitudes towards business investment may be illuminating. Additionally, it is possible that Asians answered the racial inquiry more often than Hispanics which would skew the results.

The above being said, the next step in the process is using SQL relational databases for ease of combining sources and to visualize the results with Tableau. The repository for these applications is linked below.

Application of big data and machine learning techniques form the basis of the first part of a journey to see what stories are revealed and where to focus granular analysis in order to understand more about this ongoing economic crisis at a human and macro level.
